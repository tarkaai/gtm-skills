# Create Effective LinkedIn Ad Creative

## Prerequisites
- LinkedIn campaign structure set up (see `fundamentals/social/linkedin-ads-campaign`)
- Clear value proposition and offer for your target audience

## Steps

1. **Write the ad copy.** LinkedIn Sponsored Content has: Intro Text (600 chars max, but first 150 are visible before truncation), Headline (70 chars), and Description (100 chars). Front-load your value prop in the first 150 characters. Use the headline for the specific offer or CTA. Keep description as a supporting line.

2. **Design the image.** Use a 1200x627 pixel image (1.91:1 ratio). Effective patterns: bold text overlay with 6-8 words stating the key benefit, a clean product screenshot, or a data visualization. Use high contrast and readable fonts. Avoid stock photos of people in suits -- they look generic and get scrolled past.

3. **Write for the buyer, not the product.** Bad: "Our platform uses AI to automate workflows." Good: "Stop spending 10 hours/week on manual data entry." Lead with the pain point or outcome. The prospect does not care about your features -- they care about their problem.

4. **Create 3-4 ad variants.** For each campaign, create at least 3 ad variations: different angles (pain point vs aspiration vs social proof), different images, and different CTAs. LinkedIn will auto-optimize toward the best performer. This structured testing reveals what resonates.

5. **Use social proof.** Include specific numbers: "500+ DevOps teams use Acme" or "Reduced deployment time by 73%." Customer logos (with permission) in the image drive trust. Quote a specific customer result in the intro text. Social proof is the highest-performing element in B2B ad creative.

6. **Refresh creative every 2-3 weeks.** LinkedIn audiences are relatively small, so ad fatigue sets in quickly. Monitor frequency (average impressions per person). When frequency exceeds 5, swap in new creative. Rotate the angle -- if pain-point creative fatigued, try social proof or aspirational messaging next.
